Title: Metformin in Type 2 Diabetes — Educational Summary
Note: Original synthetic summary written for demo purposes. Not a substitute for a clinical reference.

1. Background
Metformin is a biguanide oral antihyperglycemic agent and is widely recommended
as the first-line pharmacologic therapy for type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) in
adults without contraindications. It improves glycemic control primarily by
reducing hepatic gluconeogenesis and, to a lesser extent, by improving
peripheral insulin sensitivity.

2. Efficacy
In typical clinical use, metformin lowers HbA1c by approximately 1.0–1.5
percentage points when used as monotherapy in treatment-naive patients.
Compared with sulfonylureas, metformin is associated with similar or modestly
better long-term glycemic durability and a lower risk of hypoglycemia.

3. Cardiovascular and weight effects
Unlike sulfonylureas and insulin, metformin is weight-neutral or modestly
weight-lowering. Long-term observational data and the UKPDS trial suggest a
possible cardiovascular benefit in overweight patients with T2DM, though the
magnitude of effect remains debated.

4. Adverse effects
The most common adverse effects are gastrointestinal: nausea, diarrhea,
abdominal discomfort, and metallic taste. These are typically dose-dependent
and improve when the medication is taken with food and titrated slowly.
Long-term use can be associated with vitamin B12 deficiency; periodic
monitoring is reasonable in patients on extended therapy.

5. Lactic acidosis
Metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA) is rare but serious. The risk is
elevated in conditions that promote tissue hypoxia or impair drug clearance:
acute kidney injury, severe heart failure with hypoperfusion, sepsis, severe
hepatic disease, and conditions involving acute volume depletion.

6. Contraindications and cautions
- Estimated GFR (eGFR) < 30 mL/min/1.73 m² — contraindicated.
- eGFR 30–45 mL/min/1.73 m² — use with caution; consider dose reduction and
  do not initiate.
- Acute or unstable heart failure.
- Hepatic impairment with risk of lactic acidosis.
- Hold metformin around iodinated contrast administration when eGFR is
  reduced (commonly < 60 mL/min/1.73 m²) and resume after renal function is
  reassessed.

7. Dosing
Typical adult starting dose is 500 mg once or twice daily with meals,
titrated weekly as tolerated to a maximum of 2000–2550 mg/day in divided
doses. Extended-release formulations may improve tolerability.

8. Place in therapy
Recent guidelines (e.g., ADA Standards of Care) continue to support metformin
as first-line therapy for most adults with T2DM. However, in patients with
established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or
chronic kidney disease, an SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor agonist may be
preferred or added regardless of HbA1c.
